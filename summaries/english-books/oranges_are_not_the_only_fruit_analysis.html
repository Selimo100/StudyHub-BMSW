Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit

1. Quick Overview

Use this first

Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit is a semi-autobiographical coming-of-age novel about Jeanette, an adopted girl raised in a strict evangelical household in northern England. Her mother believes Jeanette has been chosen by God and trains her to become a missionary. Jeanette grows up inside a powerful church community, but when she falls in love with women, her private identity clashes with the religious story her mother and church have written for her.

Core meaning: The novel shows that identity is not something other people should force on you. Jeanette must move from an inherited story to her own story.

AuthorJeanette Winterson
First published1985
GenreSemi-autobiographical novel, Bildungsroman, lesbian fiction, postmodern fiction
NarratorFirst-person narrator: Jeanette looking back on her childhood and adolescence
StructureEight chapters named after Old Testament books
Main conflictJeanette’s sexuality and independence vs. her mother’s/church’s rigid worldview
One-sentence exam summary: The novel is about a young woman who grows up in a strict religious community and learns that love, faith and identity are more complex than the single “correct” version of life she has been taught.

2. The Whole Story in Clear Stages

1. Chosen child: Jeanette is adopted by a mother who sees her as a gift from God and a future missionary.

2. Religious childhood: Jeanette learns Bible stories, church language and a strict division between good and evil.

3. Contact with outside worlds: School, hospital and figures like Elsie show Jeanette that her mother’s worldview is not the only one.

4. Awakening desire: Jeanette falls in love with Melanie and later Katy. She experiences these relationships as love, not as sin.

5. Public judgement: The church discovers Jeanette’s relationship and interprets it as demonic or sinful.

6. Break with home: Jeanette is pushed out by her mother and community. Leaving is painful but necessary.

7. Return and distance: In the final chapter, Jeanette returns home as an adult. There is no perfect reconciliation, but she now has her own voice.

3. Detailed Plot and Chapter Analysis

Each chapter title comes from the Old Testament. Winterson uses biblical names to make Jeanette’s personal life feel like a sacred history, but she also uses them ironically.

Genesis

PlotThe novel begins with Jeanette’s childhood in a working-class town in northern England. She lives with her adoptive mother and father. Her father is almost invisible in the family; he watches wrestling and stays passive. Her mother is the dominant force: energetic, combative, dramatic and deeply religious. She divides the world into friends and enemies. Religion is not just Sunday worship; it is the organising system for everything in the house.

Jeanette’s mother has adopted her because she wants a child dedicated to God. Jeanette is not treated simply as a daughter but as a mission, a project, almost a miracle. Her mother imagines her as a future missionary. Jeanette grows up feeling special and chosen. The house is full of Bible stories, missionary maps, prayer routines, church meetings and moral judgement.

Important early moments already foreshadow Jeanette’s future conflict. A gypsy predicts that Jeanette will never marry and will never be still. Jeanette likes two women who run a paper shop, but her mother forbids contact because she suspects “unnatural” sexuality. Jeanette does not understand this yet, but the reader sees that lesbian existence is already present in the world around her and is already being policed by her mother.

Direct analysis: “Genesis” means beginning. This chapter creates the origin story of Jeanette’s identity. It also shows that the mother’s love is conditional: Jeanette is valued because she fits a religious fantasy. The sentence that Jeanette is one of the mother’s friends “at first” foreshadows that Jeanette will later be reclassified as an enemy.

Exodus

PlotJeanette has to go to school because the authorities require it. Her mother is against school because she sees it as a place where Jeanette could be corrupted. School is Jeanette’s first major movement out of the closed religious home. She does not fit in because she interprets everyday life through the Bible and missionary ideas. Teachers and pupils see her as strange.

A major episode is Jeanette’s deafness. She becomes deaf because of a medical problem, but the church misreads her silence as spiritual rapture. Miss Jewsbury is the one who realises that Jeanette is not in a holy trance but cannot hear. Jeanette goes to hospital and has an operation. Her mother brings oranges as comfort. Elsie Norris visits Jeanette in hospital and becomes one of the most important positive influences in her life. Elsie reads, tells stories, loves poetry and teaches Jeanette that reality can be interpreted in more than one way.

The chapter also includes Jeanette’s growing awareness that school and church offer different systems of knowledge. School does not fully save her from narrow thinking, but it makes her aware that her mother’s explanations are not universal.

Direct analysis: “Exodus” means departure. Jeanette does not leave home yet, but she begins leaving her mother’s absolute control. The deafness episode is powerful irony: religious certainty fails to recognise ordinary physical reality. Elsie becomes an alternative spiritual guide: still religious, but imaginative and humane.

Leviticus

PlotThis chapter is full of laws, taboos and moral policing. Jeanette’s mother and Mrs White obsess over “the Heathen”, especially the people next door. When they hear sexual noises through the wall, they respond by singing hymns loudly. The scene is comic, but it also shows how the church tries to control bodies, sex and privacy.

Jeanette’s school problems continue. She does not separate religious life from school life, so she writes and behaves in ways teachers find inappropriate. She is trained to preach and speak in church, and she becomes a confident young religious figure. Her intelligence and leadership are encouraged, but only inside strict boundaries.

The chapter also contains inserted stories and fairy-tale-like material. These interruptions are not random: they show that Jeanette’s mind is shaped by myth, Bible, fable and imagination. Winterson is teaching the reader not to expect a simple realistic autobiography.

Direct analysis: The biblical Leviticus is associated with religious law, purity and prohibition. In the novel, this chapter exposes how rules can become absurd and cruel. The humour matters because it prevents the community from being shown only as evil; they are funny, human and ridiculous, but also dangerous.

Numbers

PlotJeanette is growing older and begins thinking about sexuality, marriage and the adult world. She has unsettling dreams about marriage, where the groom may become a pig, her mother, a blind figure or even an empty suit. These dreams show that heterosexual marriage feels strange, threatening or meaningless to her.

Jeanette meets Melanie at a fish stall. She is attracted to her and brings her into the church. Melanie converts, and Jeanette becomes her spiritual guide. Their friendship becomes romantic and sexual. Jeanette does not experience this as evil; she experiences it as love and closeness. This is crucial: the church later calls the relationship sin, but the narrative shows it first as sincere human affection.

Jeanette’s mother becomes suspicious but initially misinterprets the situation as interest in a boy named Graham. The mother tells Jeanette the story of Pierre, a man from her own past in France. This story is comic and revealing: the mother once experienced desire too, but she has rewritten sexuality as danger and shame.

Pastor Finch gives a sermon about “unnatural passions”. Melanie breaks under pressure and repents publicly. Jeanette is forced into a religious crisis. The church treats her love as demonic possession and tries to correct her. Miss Jewsbury, who understands more about sexuality than the church admits, speaks to Jeanette and later becomes sexually involved with her. This moment should be analysed carefully because Miss Jewsbury is an adult and an authority figure; the scene exposes hidden contradictions within the supposedly pure religious community.

Direct analysis: This is the main rising action and first major climax. Jeanette’s inner truth collides with the public truth of the church. The chapter shows that the problem is not simply that Jeanette loves Melanie; the problem is that the church can only interpret difference as sin.

Deuteronomy

Plot / reflectionThis chapter is less plot-driven and more philosophical. Jeanette reflects on history, storytelling and truth. She questions the idea that history is objective and stories are false. She argues that all accounts are shaped by perspective. People choose what to remember, what to forget and how to organise the past.

This reflective chapter helps us understand the whole novel. Jeanette has been raised inside other people’s stories: her mother’s adoption story, the church’s moral story, the Bible’s sacred story, society’s heterosexual story. Now she begins to understand that stories are made, and if they are made, they can also be challenged.

Direct analysis: Deuteronomy in the Bible retells the law for a new generation. Winterson uses this title to retell and question Jeanette’s own history. The chapter is a manifesto for the novel’s form: fragmented stories may be more truthful than one official version.

Joshua

PlotJeanette tries to continue with church life after the crisis with Melanie. She still believes, still preaches and still has power within the church community. This is important: Jeanette does not immediately reject religion. Her conflict is with the church’s refusal to accept her whole self.

There is a flashback to the “Awful Occasion” when Jeanette’s biological mother came to claim or see her. Jeanette overhears the truth and confronts her adoptive mother, who insists that she alone is Jeanette’s real mother and calls the biological mother only a carrying case. This scene reveals how possessive the adoptive mother is. She does not want Jeanette to have any origin outside herself and God.

Jeanette becomes involved with Katy, another young woman in the church. This proves that her love for Melanie was not a temporary confusion. Melanie later returns with news of marriage to an army man. Jeanette feels betrayed and angry because Melanie has chosen social and religious acceptability. Jeanette’s relationship with Katy is discovered or becomes impossible to hide, and the pressure builds again.

Direct analysis: In the Bible, Joshua is associated with entering a promised land. In the novel, Jeanette seeks a new territory of identity, but it is not simple or safe. The chapter shows that leaving an old worldview is gradual and emotionally messy.

Judges

PlotThe title is direct: Jeanette is judged. Her mother, the pastor and much of the congregation see her as a moral threat. Jeanette understands that the conflict “hinges” on the fact that she loves the wrong kind of people according to the church. She is told or pressured to leave home. There is no easy refuge: going to Katy would create problems for Katy, and Jeanette has limited money.

Elsie’s death is one of the most painful losses. Elsie had given Jeanette emotional and imaginative support. Without Elsie, Jeanette is more isolated. The church controls the funeral and handles grief in a way that does not truly honour Jeanette’s relationship with Elsie.

Jeanette finally leaves home. She takes her books, musical instruments and Bible. This detail matters: leaving the church community does not mean she simply abandons everything. She takes language, music, faith and story with her, but she will use them differently.

Direct analysis: This is the clearest climax of rejection. Jeanette is expelled from the world that created her. The chapter is painful because freedom comes through loss, not through a triumphant victory.

Ruth

PlotThe final chapter mixes Jeanette’s adult life, return home and the fairy-tale story of Winnet Stonejar. In the Winnet story, a girl is taken in by a powerful sorcerer. He teaches her and gives her a world, but he also controls the story of who she is. When Winnet forms a bond outside his control, he casts her out. A raven tells her that she will not lose her power; she will use it differently. This clearly parallels Jeanette and her mother/church.

Jeanette works, survives and later returns home. Her mother has not completely changed. She still avoids full acceptance and uses religious language, humour and denial. However, Jeanette now has distance. She can see her mother’s absurdity, cruelty and humanity at the same time. The famous title idea appears at the end when the mother finally admits, in her own limited way, that oranges are not the only fruit.

The ending is open. Jeanette is not fully reconciled with her mother, and the past is not healed. But Jeanette is no longer trapped. She has become the storyteller of her own life.

Direct analysis: Ruth in the Bible is about loyalty, exile and choosing where to belong. Winterson’s ending asks what kind of loyalty is possible after rejection. Jeanette can return, but not as the obedient child her mother wanted.

6. Key Relationships and What They Show

Jeanette + Mother

This is the central relationship. The mother gives Jeanette identity, language, faith and purpose, but also tries to control every part of her life. Their relationship is intense because love and power are mixed together. The mother cannot accept Jeanette as separate.

Exam point: The mother is not a simple villain; she is funny, strong and sometimes caring. The tragedy is that her love only works when Jeanette obeys her story.

Jeanette + Elsie

Elsie is Jeanette’s alternative mentor. She supports Jeanette through hospital, stories and imagination. She does not offer rigid dogma but teaches Jeanette that meanings can be layered.

Exam point: Elsie shows that religion and imagination can be generous rather than controlling.

Jeanette + Melanie

Melanie is Jeanette’s first love and the trigger for the major conflict. At first, Jeanette sees their bond as compatible with faith. The church forces a contradiction between love and salvation.

Exam point: Melanie’s later conformity shows the power of shame and community pressure.

Jeanette + Katy

Katy’s relationship with Jeanette shows that Jeanette cannot be “cured”. It repeats the church conflict and proves that Jeanette’s desire is part of her identity.

Exam point: Katy is important because she turns a single scandal into a pattern the church cannot ignore.

Jeanette + Church

The church is both home and prison. Jeanette is talented and valued as long as she serves the church. When her sexuality becomes visible, the same community rejects her.

Exam point: The novel criticises conditional belonging.

Jeanette + Miss Jewsbury

Miss Jewsbury understands Jeanette better than many adults, but her sexual involvement with Jeanette is uncomfortable because of age and power. She exposes hypocrisy, but she is not a simple saviour.

Exam point: Use her to discuss ambiguity: not all alternatives to the mother are purely safe.

7. Direct Answers to All Worksheet Categories

1. Narrator + Point of View

Q1 Who is the narrator? Jeanette is the narrator. She tells her own life story retrospectively, meaning she is older than the child she describes. Her relationship to the events is deeply personal: she is both the person who suffered and the person now shaping the memory into a story.

Q2 What point of view is used and what effect does this have? The novel uses first-person narration. This creates intimacy, humour and emotional directness. We experience the world through Jeanette’s unusual childhood logic, so the church’s beliefs can seem normal at first and absurd later.

Q3 Is the narrator reliable? Jeanette is reliable emotionally but not conventionally objective. She mixes realistic memory with fairy tale, biblical structures, symbolic episodes and adult reflection. She may not always give factual history, but she gives a truthful account of how events felt and what they meant.

2. Structure + Freytag’s Pyramid

Exposition: Genesis and early Exodus: Jeanette’s home, mother, adoption, church and worldview are introduced.

Rising action: School, hospital, Elsie, church leadership, Jeanette’s growing difference, and then her relationship with Melanie.

Climax: The discovery of Jeanette’s lesbian relationship, Melanie’s repentance, the exorcism/intervention and Jeanette’s refusal to be fully “corrected”.

Falling action: Jeanette attempts to continue in the church, but her relationship with Katy and the loss of Elsie make return impossible.

Resolution: Jeanette leaves home and later returns as an adult. The ending is open: there is partial contact with the mother but no full reconciliation.

Chronology: The main story roughly follows Jeanette from childhood to adulthood, but it is interrupted by flashbacks, fairy tales, reflective essays and symbolic narratives. This makes the structure fragmented and postmodern.

3. Genre + Form

The novel is a Bildungsroman because it follows Jeanette’s growth from childhood to independence. It is also semi-autobiographical, as the protagonist shares the author’s name and broad life situation. It is lesbian fiction because it centres same-sex desire and identity. It is postmodern because it rejects a single linear form and mixes realism with myth, Bible, fairy tale and metafictional reflection.

Winterson subverts the coming-of-age genre because Jeanette does not simply grow into society. Instead, she grows away from the version of society offered to her.

4. Language + Style

Winterson’s style is comic, sharp, poetic and symbolic. She uses short humorous sentences to expose absurdity, especially in church scenes. She also uses lyrical and philosophical passages when Jeanette reflects on truth, history and storytelling. Biblical language gives ordinary events a mythic scale, while local working-class speech keeps the novel grounded in a real community.

Syntax effect: Fast comic scenes often use quick, blunt phrasing. Reflective passages use longer, more flowing sentences to create a thoughtful, essay-like mood.

Tone shifts: The tone shifts from comedy to pain when Jeanette’s sexuality is judged, when Elsie dies and when Jeanette leaves home. These shifts show that the funny world of the church can become emotionally violent.

5. Literary Devices

Metaphor/symbol 1: Oranges. They represent the mother’s limited form of comfort and her belief that there is one right answer. The title rejects this narrowness.

Metaphor/symbol 2: Demons. Demons represent the church’s way of explaining what it fears or cannot understand, especially sexuality and independence.

Metaphor/symbol 3: The hill. The hill gives Jeanette distance and perspective. It is a place of crisis, prayer, anger and looking beyond the town.

Irony: Jeanette’s deafness is interpreted as spiritual rapture. This is situational irony because the religious explanation is completely wrong.

Recurring motifs: fruit, biblical language, journeys, chosen children, exorcism/demons, stories inside stories, outsiders, walls, missionary work, and food.

6. Theme + Message

Central theme as a complete sentence: The novel shows that people need the freedom to define their own identity, because families, religions and communities can harm individuals when they allow only one acceptable story.

Secondary themes: religious control, sexuality, adoption, mother-daughter conflict, storytelling, class, gender roles, community, exile, memory and the difference between love and possession.

Moral position: The novel clearly criticises cruelty, hypocrisy and forced conformity, but it is not simplistic. It also shows the comfort of faith, the humour of community and the complexity of the mother.

7. Characters + Relationships

Jeanette’s characterisation: She is characterised indirectly through her actions and voice: she knows the Bible, preaches, jokes, questions, loves deeply and refuses to disappear. She is both obedient and rebellious.

Change: She changes from a child who accepts her mother’s categories to a young woman who recognises that those categories cannot contain her. She does not lose everything from childhood; she transforms it.

Key relationship: Jeanette and her mother drive the whole plot. The mother gives Jeanette a world, but Jeanette must leave that world to survive.

8. Setting + Context

The setting is a northern English working-class town with a strict evangelical church community. The atmosphere is claustrophobic because everyone knows everyone, private life becomes public and religious judgement enters ordinary domestic spaces.

The social and historical context matters because lesbian identity is treated as sin or illness rather than as a valid identity. The church offers women power in some ways, but only inside strict moral limits.

Symbolically, the town represents a closed world; the hill represents perspective; journeys represent escape; the church represents both belonging and control.

9. Personal Reception + Opinion

Most compelling character: A strong answer is Elsie, because she is kind, eccentric and intellectually free. She helps Jeanette understand that imagination is a form of survival.

Greatest emotional impact: Jeanette leaving home, because she loses family, community, dog and childhood identity at once. The effect comes from understatement: it is not melodramatic, which makes it feel more real.

Possible confusing point: The fairy-tale sections can feel separate at first, but they mirror Jeanette’s emotional life and help explain what realistic narration cannot.

10. Broader Significance

The novel is significant because it combines working-class realism, lesbian coming-of-age, religious satire and postmodern narrative experimentation. It stands out because it tells a queer story without presenting sexuality as tragedy or illness, while still showing the real pain caused by social rejection.

Its themes remain relevant today: identity, coming out, religious family conflict, chosen family, mental health, and the pressure to fit into one approved life path.

Recommendation to a peer: Read it if you want a book that is funny and painful at the same time, and if you are interested in identity, religion, family and the power of telling your own story.

8. Themes in Depth

Identity vs. imposed identity

Jeanette’s mother decides Jeanette’s purpose before Jeanette can choose. The novel follows Jeanette’s struggle to move from being a “chosen child” in someone else’s story to being a self-defined adult.

Religion: faith vs. control

The novel does not simply reject religion. Jeanette genuinely loves parts of church life. The criticism is aimed at rigid religion that turns love into sin and difference into demonic possession.

Sexuality and shame

Jeanette experiences love for women as natural and meaningful. The shame comes from the community’s interpretation, not from the love itself.

Motherhood and possession

The mother’s adoption of Jeanette is presented almost like a religious calling. This makes the relationship intense, but also possessive. Jeanette is expected to belong to her mother completely.

Storytelling and truth

The book argues that truth is not only found in official history. Fairy tales, myths and personal memories can reveal emotional truths that ordinary factual accounts hide.

Community and exclusion

The church gives Jeanette belonging and status, but the belonging is conditional. Once Jeanette no longer fits, the community becomes a system of judgement.

Class and local culture

The working-class town is full of humour, gossip, shops, food, church events and local speech. This gives the novel warmth and realism, even when the central conflict is painful.

Leaving and returning

Jeanette’s freedom requires departure, but the final return shows that the past is never simply gone. She can revisit it because she is no longer trapped inside it.

10. Narrative Technique: Why the Book Feels Unusual

Fragmented structure

The novel does not only tell events in order. It interrupts the plot with fairy tales, reflections and symbolic stories. This reflects the idea that identity is built from many narratives, not one neat timeline.

Humour and pain together

Winterson often makes religious scenes funny before showing their cruelty. This makes the community feel alive and human, not flat. It also makes the later rejection more painful.

Child perspective + adult insight

Jeanette often describes childhood events as she understood them then, but with adult irony. This double perspective is why the narration feels both innocent and clever.

Metafiction

The novel thinks about storytelling while telling a story. It asks who controls the past and whether official history is more truthful than personal memory.

11. Ready-to-Use Exam Answers

Short oral answers you can learn

Narrator: “The story is told by Jeanette as a first-person narrator looking back on her childhood. This makes the novel intimate and subjective, because we see events through memory, humour and imagination.”

Main theme: “The central theme is self-definition. Jeanette is raised to become her mother’s religious ideal, but she gradually discovers that her identity cannot be reduced to one approved story.”

Religion: “The novel does not simply attack religion. It shows that faith can give language and community, but it becomes destructive when it refuses love, complexity and difference.”

Mother: “Jeanette’s mother is both comic and frightening. She gives Jeanette purpose, but she also tries to possess her and cannot accept her as separate.”

Oranges: “Oranges symbolise the mother’s limited worldview. They are a form of comfort, but they also represent the idea that there is only one acceptable answer. The title rejects that idea.”

Structure: “The Old Testament chapter titles make Jeanette’s life resemble a sacred journey, but Winterson uses this structure creatively and ironically to question authority.”

Ending: “The ending is open and realistic. Jeanette returns home, but not as the obedient child. She has distance, independence and her own voice.”

12. Possible Oral Exam Questions and Strong Points

Possible questionWhat to answer
How does Jeanette change?From chosen obedient child to independent narrator. She does not simply reject her past; she transforms its language and stories for herself.
Is the mother a villain?No, not only. She is controlling and cruel, but also funny, strong and shaped by her own disappointments. This complexity makes her realistic.
Why are fairy tales included?They mirror Jeanette’s emotional reality. Winnet’s story especially explains exile, power and leaving home symbolically.
How is religion presented?As both meaningful and dangerous. It gives community and imagination, but rigid religious authority becomes harmful when it denies Jeanette’s identity.
What does the title mean?It means that there is not only one form of comfort, truth, love or life. The mother offers oranges as if they are enough; Jeanette learns there are other possibilities.
What is the role of Elsie?Elsie is a mentor and emotional safe place. She represents imagination, literature and a kinder form of spirituality.

13. Final Reflection: Perfect Exam Version

In one or two sentences, what is the book about?
The book is about Jeanette, an adopted girl raised in a strict evangelical community, who grows up believing she is chosen by God but later discovers that her sexuality and independence do not fit the role chosen for her. On a deeper level, it is about how people survive by rewriting the stories that family, religion and society impose on them.

What is the single most important thing to say?
The novel’s most important idea is that there is never only one story, one truth or one acceptable way to live. Jeanette’s freedom begins when she becomes the narrator of her own life.

One passage to discuss in depth:
The deafness episode is excellent because it combines humour, irony, religion, childhood perspective and the theme of misinterpretation. The church thinks Jeanette is spiritually transformed, but she is medically deaf. This shows the danger of forcing reality into a religious explanation.

The Winnet Stonejar story is ideal for analysing structure and symbolism. Winnet’s relationship with the sorcerer mirrors Jeanette’s relationship with her mother and church: love, teaching and power are mixed with control, punishment and exile.
