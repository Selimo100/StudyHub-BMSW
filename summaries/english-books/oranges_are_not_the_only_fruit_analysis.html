AuthorJeanette Winterson
Publication1985
GenreSemi-autobiographical coming-of-age novel
SettingNorthern England, evangelical community

1. Book overview

Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit tells the story of Jeanette, an adopted girl raised by a powerful Pentecostal mother in a strict religious community in northern England. Jeanette grows up believing that she is chosen by God and destined to become a missionary, but as she becomes a teenager, her love for women brings her into conflict with her mother, her church and the identity they have created for her.

Basic story in simple English

Jeanette is adopted by a deeply religious mother who wants to raise her as a servant of God. As a child, Jeanette lives inside the world of church, Bible stories, missionary work and strict moral categories. School, hospital and people like Elsie gradually show her that there are other ways to understand life. When Jeanette falls in love with Melanie, and later Katy, the church interprets her sexuality as sin or demonic possession. Jeanette is judged, pressured and eventually pushed out. As an adult, she returns home, but she is no longer the obedient child: she has become the narrator of her own life.

What this book is really about

The novel is about the struggle between imposed identity and self-definition. Jeanette’s mother, church and community tell her that there is only one correct story: God has chosen her, heterosexual marriage is normal, and anything else is sinful. Jeanette’s journey shows that this single story is not enough. The title means that there are other kinds of love, truth, comfort and life.

Core interpretation for the exam: Winterson criticises systems that only accept one version of identity. The novel does not simply reject religion; it shows that faith, stories and community can be powerful, but they become destructive when they are used to deny complexity, sexuality and personal freedom.

2. General summary to memorise

Memorisable version: Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit is about Jeanette, an adopted girl who is raised in a strict evangelical household in northern England. Her mother believes Jeanette is chosen by God and trains her to become a missionary. At first, Jeanette accepts this worldview: the church is her home, the Bible gives her language, and she feels special. But when she starts school, spends time in hospital and meets people like Elsie, she learns that her mother’s world is not the only possible world. As a teenager, Jeanette falls in love with Melanie and later Katy. She experiences these relationships as genuine love, but the church calls them sinful and tries to “correct” her through confession, prayer and exorcism-like treatment. Jeanette is forced to choose between belonging to the community and being honest about herself. Eventually, she leaves home and later returns as an adult. The ending is not a perfect reconciliation, but Jeanette has gained distance, independence and narrative control. The deeper message is that no family, religion or society should be allowed to force a person into only one story: oranges are not the only fruit.

Beginning

Jeanette is a “chosen” child, adopted to fulfil her mother’s religious dream. Her identity is defined before she can define herself.

Middle

Jeanette’s encounters with school, Elsie, Melanie and Katy make her realise that love, truth and identity are more complex than her church allows.

End

Jeanette is judged and leaves, but she does not lose everything. She transforms the stories, faith and language of her childhood into her own voice.

3. Detailed plot summary by chapter

Each chapter title comes from the Old Testament. Winterson uses biblical names to make Jeanette’s personal life sound like a sacred history, but she also uses them ironically to question religious authority.

GenesisExodusLeviticusNumbersDeuteronomyJoshuaJudgesRuth
GenesisBeginning

Childhood, adoption and the mother’s world

What happens

Jeanette introduces her childhood home. She lives with her adoptive mother and father in a working-class town. Her father is passive and almost invisible; her mother is dominant, dramatic, religious and combative. Jeanette’s mother divides the world into friends and enemies, including God, the Devil, neighbours, sex, slugs and missionary work. Jeanette explains that she was adopted because her mother wanted a child dedicated to the Lord.

Why it matters

This chapter creates Jeanette’s origin story. She is not treated as an ordinary child but as a mission and a miracle. Her mother’s love is real in some ways, but it is also conditional: Jeanette is valued because she fits the mother’s religious fantasy.

Character development

Jeanette begins as obedient and special. The mother is established as the central power in the family. The father’s passivity shows that this is not a traditional father-led household; the mother and church shape Jeanette most strongly.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Adoption, chosen identity, religion, mother-daughter power, binary thinking, missionary imagination, early hints of sexuality through the paper-shop women and the gypsy’s prediction that Jeanette will never marry.

Short analysis: “Genesis” means beginning. The chapter shows that Jeanette’s life is already being narrated by someone else. Her mother gives Jeanette a story before Jeanette can choose one. The phrase that Jeanette is one of the mother’s friends “at first” foreshadows that she will later become an enemy when she no longer fits the mother’s categories.
ExodusDeparture

School, deafness, hospital and Elsie

What happens

Jeanette has to go to school because the authorities require it. Her mother calls school a dangerous “Breeding Ground” and fears it will corrupt Jeanette. At school, Jeanette does not fit in because she explains everything through Bible stories and church logic. A major episode follows: Jeanette becomes deaf because of a medical problem, but the church interprets her silence as holy rapture. Miss Jewsbury finally realises Jeanette cannot hear and takes her to hospital. Jeanette has an operation and recovers. Her mother brings oranges; Elsie Norris visits often and tells stories, reads poetry and comforts her.

Why it matters

Jeanette’s first “exodus” is not a complete escape, but it is a movement out of her mother’s closed world. School and hospital introduce other authorities: teachers, doctors, Miss Jewsbury and Elsie. The deafness episode exposes the danger of interpreting every physical reality through religion.

Character development

Jeanette begins to realise that adults can be wrong. Elsie becomes an alternative mentor: religious, but imaginative, gentle and intellectually open. The mother’s limitations become clearer because she cannot see what Jeanette needs emotionally.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Exodus, misinterpretation, religious certainty versus reality, education, alternative mentorship, oranges as comfort, storytelling as healing.

Short analysis: The chapter is comic and painful at once. The church’s mistake about Jeanette’s deafness is situational irony: they think she is spiritually chosen, but she is medically ill. Elsie’s statement that stories help people understand the world is one of the novel’s key ideas.
LeviticusLaw

Rules, taboos and moral policing

What happens

This chapter focuses on religious rules, social taboos and the absurdity of moral judgement. Jeanette’s mother and Mrs White worry about neighbours, sexuality and “the Heathen”. When they hear sexual noises through the wall, they respond with hymns and religious outrage. Jeanette also continues to struggle at school because she cannot separate religious life from everyday expectations. She becomes more active in church, preaching and taking religious responsibility.

Why it matters

The chapter shows how the church community controls behaviour through rules, gossip and fear. It also shows that Jeanette is powerful inside the church: she is not only oppressed by it, but also trained by it and valued by it.

Character development

Jeanette becomes more confident as a preacher and religious speaker. The mother and Mrs White become comic but threatening figures of moral surveillance.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Law, purity, sexual taboo, religious comedy, hypocrisy, community control, public versus private life.

Short analysis: Biblical Leviticus is associated with law and purity. Winterson uses the title to show how rules can become absurd and cruel. Humour is important here: the religious community is ridiculous and human, but its ridiculousness can still become harmful when it judges Jeanette.
NumbersTesting

Melanie, desire and the first major crisis

What happens

Jeanette grows older and begins to think about sexuality, marriage and adulthood. She has strange dreams about marriage, suggesting that heterosexual marriage feels threatening or empty to her. She meets Melanie at a fish stall and is drawn to her. Jeanette brings Melanie into the church and Melanie converts. Their friendship becomes romantic and sexual. Jeanette experiences the relationship as love, not sin. Her mother becomes suspicious but initially misreads the situation as interest in a boy called Graham. Pastor Finch preaches against “unnatural passions”; the church discovers or confronts the relationship; Melanie breaks under pressure and repents publicly. Jeanette is treated as spiritually wrong and is subjected to prayer, judgement and exorcism-like intervention. Miss Jewsbury speaks to Jeanette and later becomes sexually involved with her, revealing hidden contradictions inside the church.

Why it matters

This is the main turning point. Jeanette’s private truth collides with the church’s public truth. The church can only interpret her love through sin, demons and correction, while the narration presents it as sincere and human.

Character development

Jeanette begins to separate her own understanding of love from the church’s interpretation. Melanie is a catalyst but cannot resist pressure. The mother starts to see Jeanette as a threat instead of only a blessing. Miss Jewsbury becomes more ambiguous.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Sexual awakening, queer love, shame, confession, exorcism, religious control, mother-daughter conflict, hypocrisy, desire versus doctrine.

Short analysis: “Numbers” suggests testing, counting and wandering. Jeanette is tested by desire and by the community. A key exam point is that the novel does not present lesbian love as inherently tragic; the tragedy comes from the church’s response and from Melanie’s fear.
DeuteronomyRetelling

History, memory and storytelling

What happens

This chapter is more reflective than plot-driven. Jeanette thinks about history, truth and storytelling. She questions the idea that official history is objective and that stories are merely false. She suggests that all histories are shaped, selected and narrated.

Why it matters

This chapter explains how to read the whole novel. Jeanette has been raised inside other people’s stories: her mother’s adoption story, the Bible’s sacred story, the church’s moral story and society’s heterosexual story. Now she starts to show that stories can be rewritten.

Character development

Jeanette becomes more clearly a storyteller, not only a character. She begins to claim authority over how her life is narrated.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

History, memory, narrative control, official truth versus personal truth, metafiction, retelling.

Short analysis: Deuteronomy retells the law. Winterson uses the title to show that retelling is not secondary; it is a form of power. The chapter is almost a manifesto for the novel’s fragmented form.
JoshuaNew territory

After Melanie, the birth mother, Katy and repeated conflict

What happens

Jeanette tries to continue in the church after the Melanie crisis. She still believes, preaches and participates. This is important because she does not instantly reject religion. A flashback reveals the “Awful Occasion”, when Jeanette’s biological mother appeared or tried to make contact. Jeanette’s adoptive mother rejects her claim and insists that she alone is Jeanette’s real mother, reducing the biological mother to a container. Jeanette later becomes involved with Katy, another young woman connected to the church. Melanie returns with news that she will marry a man in the army, and Jeanette feels anger and betrayal. The relationship with Katy shows that Jeanette’s sexuality was not a temporary mistake.

Why it matters

The chapter shows that Jeanette cannot simply be corrected. It also reveals the adoptive mother’s possessiveness: she wants to control Jeanette’s origin as well as her future.

Character development

Jeanette becomes more aware that she cannot belong to the church on its terms. Katy represents a less complicated love than Melanie. The mother becomes more controlling because her story about Jeanette is threatened.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Identity, adoption, origins, possessive motherhood, repeated desire, betrayal, impossible return to innocence.

Short analysis: In the Bible, Joshua is linked to entering a promised land. In the novel, Jeanette seeks new territory, but it is emotionally messy. She is not simply leaving faith behind; she is trying to find a way to exist honestly.
JudgesJudgement

Condemnation, Elsie’s death and leaving home

What happens

Jeanette is judged by her mother, pastor and church community. Her love for women is treated as a moral danger. The pressure becomes unbearable. Elsie dies, which removes Jeanette’s safest and most imaginative adult ally. The church controls the funeral and grief in ways that do not fully honour what Elsie meant to Jeanette. Jeanette eventually leaves home. She takes her books, musical instruments and Bible, but cannot take the dog, which intensifies the pain of separation.

Why it matters

This is the clearest climax of rejection. Freedom comes through loss. Jeanette is not triumphantly liberated; she is forced out of the world that formed her.

Character development

Jeanette chooses survival and honesty over conditional belonging. The mother refuses to accept Jeanette’s full self. Elsie’s death makes Jeanette’s isolation deeper.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Judgement, exile, grief, conditional love, community rejection, leaving home, survival.

Short analysis: The title is direct: Jeanette is judged. The important detail is what she takes with her. She does not abandon books, music or the Bible; she carries parts of her past into a new life and will use them differently.
RuthReturn

Adult return, Winnet Stonejar and unresolved belonging

What happens

The final chapter mixes Jeanette’s adult life, her return home and the fairy-tale story of Winnet Stonejar. In the Winnet story, a girl is taken in by a powerful sorcerer who teaches her and gives her a world, but also controls the story of who she is. When Winnet forms a bond outside his control, he casts her out. A raven tells her that her power cannot be taken back, only used differently. This clearly mirrors Jeanette’s relationship with her mother and church. Jeanette later returns home. Her mother has not fully changed; she still uses humour, denial and religion. Yet Jeanette now has distance and her own voice.

Why it matters

The ending refuses a simple happy reconciliation. Jeanette can return, but she cannot return as the obedient child. The past remains painful, but it no longer owns the whole story.

Character development

Jeanette becomes the narrator of her own life. The mother remains complex: funny, stubborn, limited and still unable to offer full acceptance.

Themes / symbols / conflicts

Loyalty, exile, return, unresolved family, storytelling, symbolic doubles, power, “oranges are not the only fruit.”

Short analysis: Ruth in the Bible is about loyalty and chosen belonging. Winterson asks what loyalty means after rejection. The ending is open but not hopeless: Jeanette has not erased her past, but she has stopped being trapped inside her mother’s version of it.

5. Narrator and point of view

Who is the narrator?

Jeanette is the narrator. She tells her own life story in the first person, looking back on childhood, adolescence, rejection and return. She is both the child in the story and the adult who shapes the story.

Point of view

The point of view is first-person retrospective narration. The reader experiences events through Jeanette’s memories, humour, imagination and later reflection.

Effect on the reader

The first-person voice creates intimacy and makes Jeanette’s strange religious childhood feel normal at first. Later, adult irony helps the reader see absurdity and cruelty. The narration is funny and painful at the same time.

Reliability

Jeanette is emotionally reliable but not conventionally objective. She mixes realistic memory with fairy tale, biblical structure, fantasy and philosophical reflection. She may not give a neutral historical record, but she gives a truthful account of how events felt and what they meant.

Exam formulation: I would not call Jeanette simply unreliable. I would say she challenges the idea that one “objective” version of history exists. Her narration shows that personal memory, myth and storytelling can reveal emotional truth.

6. Structure and Freytag’s Pyramid

Resolution
Ruth: adult return, no full reconciliation, Jeanette owns her story.
Falling action
Joshua and Judges: Katy, Elsie’s death, rejection and leaving home.
Climax
Numbers: Melanie relationship discovered, repentance and exorcism-like judgement.
Rising action
Exodus to Numbers: school, hospital, Elsie, church role, sexuality awakening.
Exposition
Genesis: adoption, mother, church world and binary worldview.

Chronology

The main life story roughly moves from childhood to adulthood, but the order is interrupted by memories, reflective essays, Bible-like chapter titles, fantasy stories and symbolic narratives.

Non-linear elements

Flashbacks explain adoption and the birth mother. Fairy tales such as Winnet Stonejar interrupt realism and mirror Jeanette’s emotional life. Deuteronomy pauses the plot to discuss history and storytelling.

Why the structure matters

The fragmented form matches the theme: identity is not one neat official history. Jeanette’s life is made from Bible stories, family myths, church rules, local gossip and her own retelling.

Ending and beginning

The novel begins with Jeanette being chosen and narrated by her mother. It ends with Jeanette returning as an adult who can narrate the mother and the past from a distance.

7. Genre and form

Bildungsroman

The book is a coming-of-age novel because it follows Jeanette’s growth from childhood to independence. However, she does not simply mature into society; she grows away from the version of society offered to her.

Semi-autobiographical fiction

The narrator shares the author’s name and broad life situation, but the book is not a simple factual autobiography. Winterson turns life into art through structure, myth and symbolic storytelling.

Postmodern / queer fiction

The novel mixes realism, Bible, fairy tale, myth, humour and metafiction. It centres lesbian identity without presenting it as illness or moral failure.

Genre conventions challenged: A traditional coming-of-age novel often shows a young person joining adult society. Here, maturity means refusing the only adult role the community offers: heterosexual religious obedience.

8. Language and style

Overall style

Winterson’s style is comic, sharp, poetic and symbolic. Everyday scenes are often funny and local, while reflective passages become philosophical and lyrical.

Biblical language

The Old Testament chapter titles give Jeanette’s life a sacred shape: beginning, departure, law, judgement, exile and return. This biblical frame is serious and ironic at the same time.

Sentence structure and pace

Comic scenes often use short, direct sentences and quick contrasts. Reflective passages use longer sentences and abstract language, slowing the pace and making the reader think about truth, history and storytelling.

Tone shifts

The tone moves from humorous childhood observation to emotional pain when Jeanette’s love is condemned, Elsie dies and Jeanette leaves home. This shift shows that a comic community can also become cruel.

9. Literary devices

Oranges as symbol: Oranges represent the mother’s limited comfort and one-answer worldview. They are loving but insufficient. The title rejects the idea that there is only one valid choice.
Biblical chapter titles: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, Joshua, Judges and Ruth transform Jeanette’s life into a sacred journey while also questioning biblical authority.
Demons as metaphor: Demons represent the church’s way of explaining what it fears, especially sexuality and independence. Difference is turned into possession.
Winnet Stonejar as allegory: Winnet mirrors Jeanette. The sorcerer mirrors the mother/church. The story explains exile, power and transformation symbolically.
Irony: Jeanette’s deafness is interpreted as spiritual rapture. The church’s certainty is comic because it is completely wrong.
Repetition: Repeated fruit, journeys, church language, enemies/friends and Bible references show how Jeanette’s mind is shaped by recurring stories.
Contrast: The novel contrasts church and school, faith and control, motherly care and possession, realistic scenes and fantasy stories.
Metafiction: The novel comments on storytelling while telling Jeanette’s story. It asks who gets to decide what counts as truth.
Humour: Religious scenes are often funny, which makes the community vivid and prevents the novel from becoming a simple attack.
Foreshadowing: The gypsy’s prediction and the paper-shop women foreshadow Jeanette’s future rejection of marriage and her attraction to women.

10. Themes and message

Central theme as a full sentence

The novel shows that people need the freedom to define their own identity, because families, religions and communities can harm individuals when they allow only one acceptable story.

Identity versus imposed identity

Explanation: Jeanette’s mother decides Jeanette’s purpose before Jeanette can choose. Where it appears: adoption story, missionary training, church expectations, conflict over sexuality. Characters: Jeanette, mother, church, biological mother. Exam point: Jeanette’s freedom begins when she becomes the storyteller rather than the object of someone else’s story.

Religion: faith versus control

The novel does not simply reject religion. Jeanette genuinely loves Bible stories, preaching and parts of church life. The criticism is directed at rigid religion that turns love into sin and difference into demonic possession.

Sexuality and shame

Jeanette experiences love for women as natural and meaningful. Shame is produced by the community, not by the love itself. Melanie and Katy show different responses to social pressure.

Motherhood and possession

The mother’s adoption of Jeanette is a form of love, but also possession. She wants total control over Jeanette’s origin, future and meaning. The biological mother episode makes this especially clear.

Storytelling and truth

Winterson argues that official histories are not automatically more truthful than stories. Fairy tales, myths and memories can reveal emotional truth. This theme is most explicit in Deuteronomy and the Winnet narrative.

Community and exclusion

The church gives Jeanette belonging, purpose and public importance. But this belonging is conditional. Once Jeanette no longer fits the approved story, the community becomes a system of judgement.

Ambiguous moral message

The novel clearly criticises hypocrisy and forced conformity, but it also shows the warmth, humour and meaning of community and faith. That is why the mother and church are complex rather than flat villains.

11. Setting and context

Place and social world

The novel is set mainly in a working-class town in northern England. The important spaces are Jeanette’s house, the church, school, hospital, local shops, the hill and later the world outside home.

Atmosphere

The atmosphere is warm, comic and claustrophobic. Everyone knows everyone, and private life quickly becomes public. Religious language enters ordinary domestic spaces.

Historical / social context

The novel reflects a time and community in which lesbian identity is treated as sin, illness or moral danger. Women can have power in the church, but only if they obey strict religious and sexual rules.

Symbolic setting

The town symbolises a closed world. The hill gives perspective and distance. The church is both home and prison. Journeys to school, hospital and away from home mark Jeanette’s gradual movement into other worlds.

13. Teacher worksheet - exam-ready answers

Narrator + Point of View

Jeanette is the first-person retrospective narrator. She is both the child experiencing events and the adult shaping them into a story. This creates intimacy, humour and subjectivity. She is emotionally reliable but not conventionally objective because she mixes memory, myth, Bible, fairy tale and reflection.

Structure + Freytag’s Pyramid

Exposition: Genesis introduces adoption, mother, church and worldview. Rising action: school, hospital, Elsie, church leadership and Jeanette’s awakening desire. Climax: the Melanie relationship is discovered and Jeanette is judged. Falling action: Jeanette tries to continue, Katy repeats the conflict, Elsie dies and Jeanette leaves. Resolution: adult Jeanette returns home without full reconciliation. The chronology is roughly linear but interrupted by flashbacks, reflections and symbolic stories.

Genre + Form

The novel is a Bildungsroman, semi-autobiographical fiction, queer coming-of-age story and postmodern novel. It follows coming-of-age conventions but subverts them because Jeanette matures by refusing the adult role expected by her community.

Language + Style

Winterson’s style is comic, poetic, biblical and reflective. She uses local speech and absurd humour in church scenes, lyrical language in symbolic passages and philosophical reflection when discussing history and storytelling. Tone shifts from comedy to pain when Jeanette’s sexuality is condemned.

Literary Devices

Key devices include oranges as a symbol of limited comfort, demons as a metaphor for feared difference, biblical chapter titles as structural allusion, irony in the deafness episode, and fairy-tale allegory in the Winnet Stonejar story.

Theme + Message

The central theme is self-definition against imposed stories. Secondary themes include religion and control, sexuality and shame, motherhood and possession, storytelling and truth, community and exclusion, class and local culture. The moral position is critical of cruelty and hypocrisy, but it is not simplistic because faith and community also have value.

Characters + Relationships

Jeanette is characterised as intelligent, imaginative and stubborn. She changes from a chosen child into an independent narrator. The central relationship is Jeanette and her mother: it drives the plot and shows how love can become possession. Elsie, Melanie, Katy, Miss Jewsbury and the church are also essential relationships.

Setting + Context

The setting is a northern English working-class town and evangelical church community. The mood is claustrophobic because private life is constantly watched. The social context shapes Jeanette’s conflict because lesbian love is treated as sin. Symbolically, the town is a closed world, the hill is perspective, the church is both home and prison, and journeys represent escape.

Personal Reception + Opinion

A strong personal answer could say: I found Elsie most compelling because she is kind, eccentric and spiritually open. The most emotional moment is Jeanette leaving home because she loses family, church, dog and childhood identity at once. The fairy-tale sections may be confusing at first, but they reveal emotional truth.

Broader Significance

The novel is significant because it combines working-class realism, lesbian coming-of-age, religious satire and postmodern narrative experimentation. It remains relevant in discussions about coming out, religious family conflict, identity, chosen family and who has the right to define someone’s life.

Final Reflection

In one or two sentences: Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit is about an adopted girl raised to fulfil her mother’s religious dream, who must leave that single story in order to live truthfully. Its deeper meaning is that identity cannot be reduced to one approved form of love, faith or life.

14. Oral exam preparation

How to talk about this book: Always connect plot to form. Do not only say “Jeanette leaves the church.” Say: “Winterson shows this through a fragmented first-person narrative that mixes biblical structure with fairy tale, which mirrors Jeanette’s struggle to rewrite the story imposed on her.”

Possible oral exam questions with sample answers

EasyWhat is the book about?

It is about Jeanette, an adopted girl raised in a strict evangelical family, who grows up believing she is chosen by God. As a teenager, she falls in love with women and is rejected by her church. On a deeper level, it is about self-definition, storytelling and the need to live beyond one imposed identity.

EasyWhat does the title mean?

The title means that there is not only one valid answer, comfort, form of love or way of life. Oranges are the mother’s repeated comfort, but Jeanette learns that this single fruit is not enough to explain the world.

MediumHow is Jeanette’s mother characterised?

She is powerful, comic, religious, controlling and possessive. She loves Jeanette in practical ways but cannot accept Jeanette as separate. Her worldview is binary: friends or enemies, God or Devil, purity or sin.

MediumIs the novel anti-religious?

Not simply. It criticises rigid religious authority and homophobia, but it also shows that faith gives Jeanette language, imagination and belonging. Elsie especially represents a kinder and more open spirituality.

MediumWhy are fairy tales included?

The fairy tales mirror Jeanette’s emotional life. The Winnet Stonejar story is especially important because it symbolises Jeanette’s relationship with her mother and church: she is taught and loved, but also controlled and finally cast out.

DifficultIs Jeanette a reliable narrator?

She is reliable emotionally, but not neutral or purely factual. She mixes memory with myth, Bible, fantasy and adult reflection. This is part of the novel’s message: truth is not only official history; personal storytelling can reveal deeper emotional truth.

DifficultWhy is the ending open rather than fully happy?

The ending is open because Jeanette returns home, but her mother has not fully changed and the past is not healed. However, Jeanette has changed: she now has distance and narrative control. She can return without surrendering.

DifficultHow does the structure support the theme?

The structure supports the theme because it rejects one simple, linear version of truth. The biblical titles show an inherited religious structure, but Jeanette rewrites that structure with her own memories and fairy tales. Form and content both show self-definition.

DifficultWhat makes the mother complex?

The mother is not simply evil. She is funny, strong and sometimes caring. She also gives Jeanette a powerful language and purpose. The tragedy is that her love becomes controlling because she can only accept Jeanette inside her own religious story.

DifficultWhy is Elsie important?

Elsie is important because she offers Jeanette another form of faith: imaginative, literary, kind and open. She shows Jeanette that stories can help people understand the world. Her death makes Jeanette’s separation from the church more painful.
