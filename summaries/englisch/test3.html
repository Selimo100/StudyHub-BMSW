Type If-clause Main clause Use

Common Traps
Verb patterns: enjoy/avoid/finish/suggest + -ing · want/decide/hope/promise + to · After prepositions, always -ing
remember to = future duty · remember doing = memory · stop smoking = quit · stop to smoke = pause
Passive: be + past participle. Present: is/are done · Past: was/were done · Perf: has been done · Future: will be done · Cont: is being done · Modal: must be done
Questions: Subject Q → no do/does/did: "Who called?" · Object Q → use do/does/did: "Who did you call?" · Embedded: "Do you know where she is?" (not "where is she")
Word Formation negatives: un- (unhappy) · im- before m/p/b (impossible) · ir- before r (irregular) · il- before l (illegal) · dis- (dishonest) · in- (insufficient)
Tense Form Key use Example
Present Simple V / V+s habits, facts, schedules She works every day.
Present Continuous am/is/are + V-ing happening now, temporary, future arrangement I am studying right now.
Past Simple V-ed / irreg. completed action at specific past time He called yesterday.
Past Continuous was/were + V-ing ongoing past action, background, interrupted She was reading when he arrived.
Present Perfect Simple have/has + past part. past→now relevance, ever/never, just/already/yet I have lost my keys.
Present Perfect Cont. have/has + been + V-ing ongoing from past until now, emphasises duration She has been working for 3 hours.
Past Perfect Simple had + past part. before another past event He had left before she arrived.
Past Perfect Continuous had + been + V-ing ongoing before another past event They had been waiting for an hour.
will-future will + V spontaneous decision, prediction, promises, offers I'll help you.
going-to future am/is/are going to + V plan/intention, evidence-based prediction She is going to visit him.
Present Cont. (future) am/is/are + V-ing fixed future arrangement We're meeting at 8.
Present Simple (future) V / V+s timetable / scheduled event The train leaves at 9.
will vs. going-to: "I'll get the door!" (spontaneous) vs "I'm going to see the dentist on Friday." (planned). Going-to also for predictions with evidence: "Look at those clouds – it's going to rain!"

State verbs describe conditions/states — NOT normally used in continuous tenses.
Category Verbs
Mental states know, believe, think*, understand, remember, forget, mean
Emotions love, hate, like, dislike, prefer, want, wish, need
Senses see, hear, smell*, taste*, feel* (involuntary)
Possession have*, own, belong, contain, consist
Appearance seem, appear, look (= seem), sound
* Dual-use verbs — can be state or action:
think: "I think you're right." (opinion = state) vs "I'm thinking about it." (considering = action)
have: "I have a car." (possession) vs "I'm having lunch." (activity)
smell/taste: "It smells nice." (state) vs "She's smelling the roses." (active)
see: "I see you." (involuntary) vs "I'm seeing the doctor." (appointment)
I am knowing the answer.
I know the answer.

Type Use If-clause Main clause Example
Zero general truths Present Simple Present Simple If you heat water, it boils.
First real / possible future Present Simple will + infinitive If it rains, I will stay home.
Second unreal / unlikely present or future Past Simple would + infinitive If I won the lottery, I would travel.
Third unreal past (regret) Past Perfect would have + past participle If I had studied, I would have passed.
⚠ Never use "would" in the if-clause!
✗ If I would know, I would tell you.
✓ If I knew, I would tell you.
could / might can replace would in the main clause:
If it stopped raining, we could go out. (= would be able to)
If I had more time, I might learn Italian. (= maybe would)
Structure Meaning Example
I wish + Past Simple regret about present (can't change now) I wish I knew the answer. (= I don't know)
I wish + Past Perfect regret about past (can't change) I wish I had studied harder.
I wish + would want sth to change / complain about ongoing situation I wish it would stop raining.
I wish + could regret inability (present) I wish I could fly.
was vs. were: After if and wish, both "was" and "were" are accepted, but "were" is more formal:
I wish I were taller. / I wish I was taller. (both ✓)
wish vs. hope: Use hope (not wish) for real, possible situations:
✗ I wish you feel better soon.
✓ I hope you feel better soon.

✗ I wish I would have a car.
✓ I wish I had a car.
Mixed Conditionals combine clauses from different conditional types to express complex time relationships.
Type If-clause Main clause Example
Past → Present Past Perfect (3rd type) would + inf. (2nd type) If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.
Present → Past Past Simple (2nd type) would have + pp (3rd type) If I weren't so lazy, I would have finished by now.
Past → Present: Unreal past cause, present result.
If she had taken the job (she didn't), she would be rich now (she isn't).

Present → Past: Unreal present state, effect on the past.
If he were more careful (he isn't), he wouldn't have crashed the car (he did).

Modal Main uses Example
can ability (present), permission (informal), possibility She can swim. Can I leave?
could ability (past), polite request, possibility, conditional He could run fast. Could you help?
must strong obligation, logical deduction (+) You must stop. She must be tired.
mustn't prohibition (strong – forbidden) You mustn't smoke here.
don't have to no obligation (optional – not necessary) You don't have to come.
should / ought to advice, recommendation, mild obligation You should see a doctor.
may permission (formal), possibility May I sit here? It may rain.
might weak / uncertain possibility She might come later.
will future, prediction, willingness, requests I will help you.
would conditional, polite request, past habit Would you mind? He would walk to school.
shall offers / suggestions (Br. Eng.) Shall I open the window?
need (to) necessity / needn't = no obligation You needn't hurry. You don't need to hurry.
mustn't vs. don't have to — these are very different!
You mustn't tell anyone. = it is forbidden / prohibited
You don't have to tell anyone. = it's optional, no obligation
Modal perfects refer to past situations and express different degrees of certainty or criticism.
Form Meaning Example
must have + pp logical deduction about the past (certain) She must have left already.
can't have + pp logical impossibility about the past He can't have done that.
should have + pp past criticism / advice (but they didn't do it) You should have called me.
shouldn't have + pp past criticism (but they did it) You shouldn't have eaten so much.
could have + pp past possibility that didn't happen She could have won.
might have + pp weak past possibility He might have missed the bus.
needn't have + pp did it but it wasn't necessary You needn't have bought flowers.

Infinitive (to + V) — after these verbs:
want, need, decide, hope, expect, plan, agree, refuse, manage, promise, seem, appear, afford, learn, fail, tend, offer, choose, would like, deserve, pretend, ask (sb)
She wants to leave. / He decided to study. / I hope to see you.
Gerund (V-ing) — after these verbs:
enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest, consider, keep, miss, practice, imagine, admit, deny, risk, involve, can't help, it's worth, give up, put off, carry on
I enjoy swimming. / She avoided talking to him. / I don't mind waiting.
Gerund after prepositions — always use -ing after any preposition:
in, at, on, of, for, to (when it's a preposition), without, after, before, by
I'm interested in learning Spanish. / She left without saying goodbye.
I'm good at cooking. / He's tired of waiting. / I look forward to hearing from you.
Special structures:
make / let + object + base verb: She made him apologise. / Let me help.
help + object + base verb or to: He helped me (to) carry it.
see / hear / watch + object + base verb or -ing: I saw her cross / crossing the road.
These verbs change meaning depending on whether they take -ing or to!
Verb + Gerund (-ing) + Infinitive (to)
stop stopped smoking = quit permanently stopped to smoke = paused another action in order to smoke
remember I remember seeing her = I have a memory of a past event Remember to call = don't forget (task in the future)
forget I forgot telling him = I don't recall that I told him I forgot to tell him = I didn't do it
try Try adding salt = experiment to see if it works Try to open it = make an effort / attempt
regret I regret saying that = I'm sorry about a past action I regret to inform you = formal, polite announcement
mean This means working harder = involves / entails I mean to finish = intend
go on She went on talking = continued the same thing She went on to talk = then moved on to a new action

Formula: be (conjugated to correct tense) + past participle
Use when: the agent is unknown, unimportant, or obvious from context. Focus is on the action, not the doer.
Tense Active example Passive form
Present Simple They make cars here. Cars are made here.
Present Continuous They are building a bridge. A bridge is being built.
Past Simple Someone stole my bag. My bag was stolen.
Past Continuous They were filming a movie. A movie was being filmed.
Present Perfect They have cancelled the flight. The flight has been cancelled.
Past Perfect Someone had eaten all the cake. All the cake had been eaten.
Future (will) They will announce the results. The results will be announced.
Going-to future They are going to demolish the building. The building is going to be demolished.
Modal You must submit the form. The form must be submitted.
Infinitive Someone needs to check this. This needs to be checked.
The agent (doer) follows by: "The cake was eaten by the dog." Only include it when it's important or surprising — don't add it just to be complete.
Reporting passives allow two equivalent structures. Common verbs: said, believed, thought, known, reported, considered, expected, claimed, alleged.
It is said that he is rich. = He is said to be rich.
It is believed that they left. = They are believed to have left.
It is reported that she will resign. = She is reported to be going to resign.
Time relation Structure after subject Example
Same time is said to be He is said to be very talented.
Before reporting is said to have + pp They are believed to have escaped.

Tense Structure Example

Prepositions go at the end in informal English:
Who did you speak to? / What are you looking for? / Which job has she applied for?
Formal: To whom did you speak?
This is one of the most common exam mistakes!
Who/What = Object Who/What = Subject
Rule Use do/does/did + inversion NO do/does/did — normal word order
Example sentence Emma phoned somebody. Somebody phoned Emma.
Question Who did Emma phone? Who phoned Emma?
More subject question examples (no do/does/did):
What happened to you? (not "What did happen")
Who broke the window? (not "Who did break")
How many people came to the party? (not "did come")
Which bus goes to the centre? (not "does go")
Rule: Positive sentence → negative tag  |  Negative sentence → positive tag
Main clause Tag Main clause Tag
She will come, won't she? Kate won't be late, will she?
He is working, isn't he? They don't like us, do they?
They have eaten, haven't they? You haven't eaten yet, have you?
You can drive, can't you? She can't come, can she?
She was there, wasn't she? He wasn't home, was he?
Special cases:
I'm right, aren't I? (NOT amn't I)
Let's go, shall we?
Don't forget, will you?
There is a problem, isn't there?

Intonation: Voice ↘ falls = you expect agreement  ·  Voice ↗ rises = genuine question
Yes/No with negative questions:
"You're not going out, are you?" → "Yes." = Yes, I am going out. / "No." = No, I'm not going out.
Embedded (indirect) questions use NORMAL word order — not question word order.
Do you know where is the station?
Do you know where the station is?
I don't know what does she want.
I don't know what she wants.
Can you tell me when did he arrive?
Can you tell me when he arrived?
Use if / whether when there is no question word:
I don't know if/whether she is coming.
He asked whether anyone had seen his keys.
Common intro phrases:
Do you know…? / Could you tell me…? / I wonder… / I don't know… / I'm not sure… / Can you tell me…? / Do you have any idea…?
